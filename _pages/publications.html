My research focuses on how mental health systems can better promote recovery and equity, particularly for people with serious mental illness and for racial and ethnic minority communities. I examine drivers of mental health service utilization and continuity of care, including stigma and discrimination, help-seeking, and culturally and linguistically appropriate services.

Across projects, I ask a common question: how do mental health systems translate recovery-oriented values into routine practice, and for whom do these translations succeed or fail? One strand of my research examines recovery-oriented practice and practitioner competencies. A second strand focuses on service access and utilization among marginalized communities, including patterns among Asian Americans and East Asian postsecondary students. A third strand centers on implementation and system design, including the integration of peer support specialists within mobile crisis teams and emergency response systems.

Methodologically, I use quantitative and computational approaches, including large-scale observational data and text-as-data methods, to produce transparent, policy-relevant evidence. My dissertation extends this work by using text-as-data methods to study the peer support workforce.
