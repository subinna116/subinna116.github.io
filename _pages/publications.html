How do mental health systems translate recovery-oriented values into routine practice, and for whom do these translations succeed or fail?

My research focuses on how mental health systems can better promote recovery and equity, particularly for people with serious mental illness and for racial and ethnic minority communities. I examine drivers of mental health service utilization and continuity of care, including stigma and discrimination, help-seeking, and culturally and linguistically appropriate services.

Methodologically, I use quantitative and computational approaches, including large-scale observational data and text-as-data methods, to produce transparent, policy-relevant evidence. My dissertation extends this work by using text-as-data methods to study the peer support workforce.

Three Strands of Inquiry

01

Recovery-Oriented Practice

How individual and organizational factors shape recovery-promoting competencies among mental health practitioners, and how recovery-oriented values are translated into clinical practice.

02

Service Access & Equity

Patterns of mental health service utilization among marginalized communities, including Asian Americans and East Asian postsecondary students, with attention to stigma, language-concordant care, and help-seeking.

03

System Design & Implementation

Integration of peer support specialists within mobile crisis teams and emergency response systems, including factors that shape implementation and the role of lived experience.
